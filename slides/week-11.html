Scope lock

Pressure-testing and cutting scope

  • Re-read the Week 10 agreed scope: the problem, the plan, the assumptions, what will NOT be built.
  • The mentor pressure-tests once more — what is the riskiest assumption?
  • Two weeks at ~20 hrs per week is a hard budget. Cut anything that does not fit.
  • Cutting is a professional skill, not a failure. Ship the smaller thing that works.
  • The intern decides what cuts to make; the mentor holds the standard.
Deadline and risk

Lock the deadline. Name the failure condition.

Lock the deadline

  • Set the exact date and time.
  • Changing it requires a re-scope conversation — not a quiet slip.
  • The deadline protects the Week 12 panel slot for the whole cohort.

Name the failure condition

  • One sentence: the single thing that, if it slipped, would make this a failure.
  • Write it down and put it somewhere visible during the build.
  • Protect that thing first above all other work this week.
Capstone types

Suitable capstones for this programme

  • A messaging-based learning helper on N8N — students or teachers ask questions via chat and get accurate, sourced answers.
  • A RAG assistant over curriculum content — searches and surfaces relevant material from real Pandai content.
  • An internal automation that removes a manual task — replaces a real, recurring human effort with a reliable automated process.
  • A small agent supporting the customer success team — handles a defined class of requests without requiring escalation.

Embedded work

Working rhythm for the build

  • Build the smallest end-to-end slice first — get something real working before adding scope.
  • Keep a running log of decisions and walls hit; it becomes your Week 12 postmortem.
  • When stuck, bring a specific, well-framed question to the mentor — not 'it does not work'.
  • Every day: what is done, what is the next smallest slice, what is blocked?
  • The mentor gets a daily one-line status — no surprises at the mid-capstone review.
Prompts for the build

Three prompts to keep moving

  • Unblocking (paste into Claude Code): 'I am stuck on [symptom]. Here is what I have tried: [list]. Here are the files and error: [paste]. Diagnose the most likely cause, propose the smallest fix, and tell me how to verify it. Do not refactor beyond what is needed.'
  • Scoping a slice: 'Here is my capstone scope and deadline: [paste]. Break it into end-to-end slices, smallest first, so I always have something working. For each slice, tell me what done looks like and what I can safely defer.'
  • Writing the runbook: 'My deployed system does [describe]. Help me write a one-page runbook: start and stop, config and secrets, three most likely failures and recovery, who to contact.'
This week's ship

Weekly project: working deployment with runbook

  • The system is deployed and works in a real environment — not a local demo.
  • Built with the real toolchain: Claude Code, GitHub, MCP server where relevant, AWS or Cloudflare, N8N.
  • Reliability checks show it works and name where it can fail.
  • A one-page runbook covers operation and recovery for a tired on-call person.
  • Scope was held — what you said you would not build, you did not build.
Mid-week checkpoint

Mid-capstone review — with the mentor

What to show

  • A working slice — something that actually runs.
  • The decision log so far.
  • Any blocker not yet resolved.

If behind

  • Re-scope now, not in Week 12.
  • The mentor confirms the new scope in writing.
  • A stuck intern caught mid-week can recover; one caught at the panel cannot.
Watch out

Pitfalls and risks

  • Scope creep mid-build: adding a related improvement instead of finishing the agreed slice. Cut it; log it for later.
  • Access blocked on day two: a credential, repo permission, or API key not confirmed in the kickoff. Confirm everything today.
  • No working slice at mid-week: building in large pieces with nothing runnable. Slice smaller and deploy earlier.
  • Waiting to surface a blocker: sitting on a stuck problem for two days before asking. Bring it to the mentor same day.
  • Runbook left to the last hour: operational docs written under pressure are incomplete. Write it as you build.
Assessment — carried into Week 12

What the panel evaluates

Real
Did the deployment actually work in a real environment?
Honest
Was the postmortem honest about what went wrong?
Reusable
Were the patterns genuinely useful to the next person?
Recap

If you remember three things

  • Scope was agreed in Week 10 — this week you build and ship it, not redesign it.
  • The mentor unblocks; the intern owns. Being rescued teaches nothing.
  • Always have a working slice. Deploy early, slice small, surface blockers same day.
