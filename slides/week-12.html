Week 12 · Phase 4 — Capstone Embedded Deployment

Ship and Defend

Present real work, defend real decisions, and leave behind something the next person can use.

The standard

The most valuable part of your presentation is the failure you almost did not mention.

— the panel, every capstone

Learning objectives

By the end of this session you have

  • Presented and defended real work under questioning from the full panel.
  • Reflected on at least one failure honestly in a written postmortem.
  • Extracted reusable patterns that help the next deployment go faster.

Agenda

Your presentation · 12–15 min

Presentation structure

  1. The problem and the scope (2 min) — what you built, who owns it, and what you chose not to build.
  2. Live demo (4 min) — real environment, real data, real trigger, real result.
  3. The decisions (3 min) — two or three real tradeoffs: model choice, integration approach, a guardrail.
  4. One production failure (3 min) — what broke, how you recovered. Do not hide this.
  5. Reusable patterns (2 min) — one or two patterns that would help the next deployment.
  6. Defence — the panel probes decisions, the failure, and the limits of the system.
Live · ~60 min total

Demos and defence

  • Each intern presents in the agreed order. Timer is visible to the room.
  • Real environment only — no localhost demos, no mock data.
  • Panel holds questions until after each presentation block is complete.
  • Defence questions come from all three panel members.
  • Audience members note questions they would ask — debrief uses them.
What the panel listens for

Signal vs. noise

Strong signal

  • Deployment worked in a real environment
  • Honest about what went wrong
  • Explains technical decisions to non-technical people
  • Owned it end to end, not carried

Weak signal

  • Demo runs only in a controlled setup
  • Failure is minimised or absent
  • Jargon without explanation
  • Credit claimed for work others carried
Written deliverable · blameless

The postmortem

  • Short summary — what you built and how it went overall.
  • Timeline — significant moments including the failure, in order.
  • Root cause — honest, focused on the system, not on blame.
  • What went well and what went badly — including where you got lucky.
  • What you would do differently — concrete enough that the next engineer benefits.

Written deliverable · pay it forward

Reusable patterns

What a pattern looks like

  • The problem it solves
  • The reusable approach
  • The pitfall to avoid

Examples

  • A reliable prompt structure for ambiguous queries
  • An integration workaround that generalises across connectors
  • A guardrail design for edge-case inputs
  • An N8N error-handling pattern for flaky APIs

Three extra capstone gates

Pass-fail questions above the rubric

  1. Did the deployment actually work in a real environment?
  2. Was the postmortem honest about what went wrong?

Recap

Graduation criteria

  • Shipped a working deliverable every week across all four phases.
  • Demonstrated calm and competence during at least one real failure.
  • Passed the capstone with a working deployment and an honest postmortem.
  • Can explain technical decisions to a non-technical audience.
