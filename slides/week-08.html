Week 08 · Phase 3 — The FDE Craft

Scoping Ambiguity

Turning a vague business problem into a concrete technical plan.

The brief

'We want to use AI.'

— every stakeholder, before the clarifying question

Learning objectives

By Friday you can

  • Turn a vague business problem into a concrete technical plan.
  • Find the real problem behind the stated one.
  • Say no to the wrong solution without losing the customer.
  • Write a clear scoping document with an explicit what-we-will-not-build section.

Why briefs are vague

Symptoms vs problems

What customers say

  • 'We want to use AI.'
  • 'Fix our support load.'
  • 'Build us a dashboard.'
  • 'Automate this process.'
  • 'Our users are dropping off.'

The cost of taking it literally

  • You build the wrong thing, correctly.
  • The real problem remains unsolved.
  • Engineering time is spent, trust is not earned.
  • The business goal was never made explicit.
Block 3 · 12 min

The clarifying question

  • What does success look like? — force a measurable outcome, not a vibe.
  • Who is the user? — a teacher, a student, an ops manager each needs a different thing.
  • What happens today without this? — reveals the workaround and the real pain.
  • What is the constraint? — time, cost, data access, existing systems.
  • Why now? — surfaces urgency, deadlines, and political context.
Live · ~12 min

Ten questions from one brief

  • Instructor hands out one-line briefs — one per pair.
  • Pairs generate ten clarifying questions in two minutes. No solutions yet.
  • Class shares: which questions are open, which are leading?
  • Instructor highlights the two or three that would most change the solution.
  • Key lesson: good questions postpone the solution; bad questions assume it.
Block 4 · 12 min

Stated request vs real need

Stated request

  • 'Fix our support load.'
  • Assumed solution: AI chatbot.
  • Assumed cause: too many tickets.
  • Assumed fix: deflect with automation.

What it might really be

  • Docs are outdated — a writing problem.
  • Tickets are misrouted — a process problem.
  • One feature confuses everyone — a UX problem.
  • Answer: you may not need AI at all.
Block 5 · 12 min

Setting boundaries

  • Scope creep is the default — bounding takes deliberate work.
  • Write the out-of-scope list before someone asks for what is on it.
  • Phrase boundaries as a plan, not a refusal: 'in this phase we focus on X.'
  • The what-we-will-not-build section protects the team and sets honest expectations.
  • Every feature added without re-scoping steals time from the agreed deliverable.
Live · ~12 min

Pushing back without losing the customer

  • Instructor models the bad pushback: 'no' with no reason — the customer leaves.
  • Instructor models the good pushback: reason + acknowledgment + alternative.
  • Formula: 'I understand why. Here is the tradeoff. Here is what we can do.'
  • Cohort observes: what changed? Tone, structure, or content?
  • Key lesson: 'no' with a reason and an alternative builds trust; 'no' alone loses it.
Live · ~16 min

Filling the scoping document on screen

  • The real problem — stated clearly, not the surface request.
  • The proposed plan — concrete, realistic, time-bounded.
  • The assumptions — what must be true for this plan to work.
  • What we will not build — explicit, reasoned, documented.
  • Instructor narrates every decision. Cohort challenges in real time.
Connecting the dots

Linking technical work to a business outcome

  • Every scoping document must answer: why is this worth building?
  • Finish this sentence: 'This is worth building because the customer will be able to...'
  • Frame the value in terms a non-engineer cares about: time, cost, reach, risk.
  • Anchor to a number you can estimate — even a rough one is better than none.
  • If you cannot articulate the business outcome, the scope is not finished.
Self-learn · 6 to 8 hrs

This week outside class

  • Topic A: Problem framing and clarifying questions — write ten questions for a vague brief, in priority order.
  • Topic B: Stated request vs real need — work a brief back to three possible underlying problems, one of which needs no AI.
  • Topic C: Writing the scoping document — fill the template, then use the AI prompt to pressure-test it as a skeptical engineering manager.
  • Topic D: Linking to a business outcome — phrase the value in three ways, each anchored to a number you can estimate.
Weekly project · 6 to 8 hrs

The deliverable

Given a deliberately vague one-line brief, produce a scoping document that states the real problem, a proposed plan, the assumptions, and explicitly what you would not build.

  • Write your clarifying questions first. Resist designing before you scope.
  • State the real problem clearly — not the surface request.
  • List assumptions explicitly. Unstated assumptions are hidden risks.
  • Write a thoughtful what-we-will-not-build section — this is graded most heavily.
  • Link the work to a business outcome a non-engineer cares about.
Assessment · 2 to 3 hrs

Defending your scope

  • Present your scoping document in class.
  • The instructor plays a customer who keeps changing the ask.
  • Hold your scope: reason, acknowledge, offer an alternative. Do not fold, do not fight.
  • Rubric: technical execution 30%, handling ambiguity 30%, communication 25%, ownership 15%.
  • Definition of done: a reader who only saw the one-line brief understands the real problem, the plan, and what is not being built.
Common mistakes

Pitfalls to avoid

  • Designing before scoping — jumping to a solution before finding the real problem.
  • A vague out-of-scope section: 'future work' is not a boundary, it is a blank cheque.
  • Unstated assumptions — every assumption you hide becomes a risk that surfaces at the worst moment.
  • Getting defensive under pressure instead of reasoning through the pushback calmly.
  • Forgetting the business outcome — a technically correct scope with no articulated value is still incomplete.
Recap

If you remember three things

  • The brief is never the real problem — your job starts with finding it.
  • The what-we-will-not-build section is where the thinking shows.
  • 'No' with a reason and an alternative builds trust; 'no' alone loses it.
