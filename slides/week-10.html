Week 10 · Phase 3 — The FDE Craft

Customer-Facing Communication and Capstone Scoping

The skill that gets faked the most — and exposed the fastest.

The real test

A non-technical decision-maker can follow your recommendation, understand the tradeoff, and believe your call.

— Week 10 definition of done

Learning objectives

By end of week you can

  • Explain technical tradeoffs to non-technical people in plain language.
  • Build trust and manage expectations before they become problems.
  • Deliver bad news clearly, early, and with a path forward.
  • Scope your own capstone so Weeks 11 and 12 are pure build and ship.
Today — 90 min

Agenda

  1. Who is in the room — framing for different audiences (8 min)
  2. Benefits over features — speak their language (10 min)
  3. Framing a tradeoff on one slide (12 min)
  4. Good vs bad: live demonstration (12 min)
  5. Managing expectations and delivering bad news (12 min)
  6. The deck and the glance test — Gemini, local context (10 min)
  7. Capstone scoping — live pairing with your mentor (18 min)
Block 1 · Who is in the room

Same fact, three audiences

What they care about

  • Executive: ROI, risk, and what happens if this goes wrong
  • Operator: reliability, daily workflow, what breaks
  • Engineer: how it works, what the failure modes are

Communicate in their terms

  • Find out who signs off before you build the slide
  • Lead with what they care about, not what you find interesting
  • One slide, one audience — never try to serve all three at once
Block 2 · Benefits over features

What it does for them, not what it does

  • Feature: the model uses retrieval-augmented generation with reranking.
  • Benefit: teachers get answers that match their school syllabus, not generic internet results.
  • Feature: latency is under 800ms at P95.
  • Benefit: students get an answer before they lose focus — no spinner, no wait.
  • Live: take one technical capability from your project and restate it as a benefit for a non-technical school administrator.
Block 3 · Framing a tradeoff

Options → cost and benefit → your recommendation

  1. State the two real options — no strawmen, no false choices.
  2. For each option: what does it cost, and what does it buy? Plain numbers, plain language.
  3. Make a clear recommendation. Say the words: 'I recommend X.'
  4. Give one reason — the strongest one. Not five reasons hedged together.
  5. Fit it on one slide. If it does not fit, the thinking is not done yet.
Block 4 · Live · ~12 min

A good and a bad version of the same conversation

  • Bad version: jargon-heavy, options listed without resolution, hedging with 'it depends,' customer leaves anxious and without a decision.
  • Good version: benefit-led, plain language, a clear recommendation with a single strong reason, customer leaves confident.
  • After each version: the class names exactly what changed.
  • What to watch for: jargon removed, recommendation added, hedge cut, one clear point per slide.
Block 5 · Managing expectations

Deliver bad news early, clearly, with a path forward

  • Set expectations before they become problems — not after the fact.
  • Bad news travels worse the longer you sit on it.
  • The structure: what happened → new date → what you are doing → what you need.
  • No excuses. Own it, even when the cause was outside your control.
  • Live: the message you send when Friday's deployment slips to the following Wednesday.
Block 6 · The deck and the glance test

One slide. One point. Five seconds.

  • The title is the conclusion, not the topic — 'Cheaper model risks accuracy drop' beats 'Model comparison.'
  • Build in Google Slides; use Gemini to draft and tighten each slide message.
  • Ask Gemini: 'Cut this to one sentence a school principal can act on.'
  • English settings: direct recommendation is expected and respected.
  • Bahasa Malaysia settings: indirect framing of problems preserves the relationship.
  • Mandarin settings: preserve the client's face — deliver hard news privately before the group meeting.
Block 7 · Capstone Scoping — last 20 min

Agree your scope before the build weeks begin

The four fields (Week 8 template)

  • Problem: one sentence — what breaks without this?
  • Plan: what you will build and in what order.
  • Assumptions: what must be true for this to work.
  • What you will NOT build: explicit, agreed, in writing.

Live mentor pairing

  • Each intern pairs with their assigned capstone mentor now.
  • Walk through the four fields together — the intern leads.
  • Mentor cuts anything that will not fit in two weeks at 20 hrs/week.
  • Leave with a scope both sides have agreed is realistic.
How this week is scored

Assessment rubric

30%
Technical execution — is the recommendation sound and the tradeoff real?
30%
Handling ambiguity — how you handle pushback and questions you cannot fully answer.
25%
Communication — clarity and trust. Can a non-technical person follow and believe it?
15%
Ownership — did you take a clear position and own the recommendation?
Weekly project — two deliverables

What you will build and submit

Deliverable 1 — Recommendation presentation

  • A real tradeoff: cost vs quality, or speed vs safety.
  • A clean Google Slides deck that passes the glance test.
  • A one-page written brief of the recommendation.
  • Live delivery scored on clarity and trust, not just technical correctness.

Deliverable 2 — Agreed capstone scope

  • One page: problem, plan, assumptions, what you will not build.
  • Agreed and signed off by your mentor in today's pairing session.
  • Realistic for two weeks at approximately 20 hours per week.
  • This scope is the contract that protects Weeks 11 and 12.
Self-learn — 6 to 8 hrs

Four topics, four Claude prompts

Topics A and B

  • A · Mixed audiences (1.5–2 hrs): Stanford and Lucidchart readings; ask Claude to rewrite a technical decision in five plain sentences leading with the benefit.
  • B · Framing tradeoffs (1.5 hrs): turn one real tradeoff into a single slide; ask Claude to frame it for one executive and one engineer simultaneously.

Topics C and D

  • C · Delivering bad news (1 hr): write the deadline-slip message; ask Claude to show both the trust-building and the trust-damaging version so you can see the difference.
  • D · Deck and capstone scope (1.5–2 hrs): tighten each slide with Gemini; apply the Week 8 template to your capstone and get it agreed with your mentor.
Watch out

Common pitfalls this week

  • Ending with 'it depends' instead of a recommendation — that is a deferral, not a communication.
  • Using jargon as a proxy for expertise — it reads as poor preparation to a non-technical audience.
  • Building a deck for engineers when the decision-maker is an executive.
  • Over-scoping the capstone — if the mentor cuts it, listen and accept the cut without negotiating it back up.
  • Sitting on bad news hoping it resolves itself — it never does, and the trust damage compounds with every day of silence.
Recap

If you remember three things

  • Communicate in the terms the listener already cares about — benefits, not features; their risk, not your architecture.
  • A recommendation without a clear call is not a recommendation — say 'I recommend X because Y' and own it.
  • Walk out today with a capstone scope your mentor has agreed is realistic — Weeks 11 and 12 depend on it.
